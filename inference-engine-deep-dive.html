Part I — The Machine

// 01 — From Text to Numbers

You type a question. The model reads it. But a neural network doesn't read English — it reads numbers. Before a single matrix multiplication fires, the text must be translated: split into pieces the model recognises (tokens), then converted into vectors of numbers the model can compute with (embeddings). This chapter is about that translation — the invisible step that happens before everything else.

Why Not Characters?

There's a fundamental tradeoff between vocabulary size and sequence length. Character-level encoding gives a tiny vocab (256 bytes) but long sequences — a single sentence might be 40 tokens. Word-level encoding gives short sequences but a vocabulary of 500K+ entries, and it can't handle new words like "ChatGPT" or "defenestration." Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) is the sweet spot: a learned vocabulary of subword pieces that balances both.

Comparison
Approach        Vocab       "unhappiness" →         "The capital of France is Paris"
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Characters      256         u,n,h,a,p,p,i,n,e,s,s   30 tokens   (long sequence)
BPE (GPT-2)     50,257      un|happ|iness            8 tokens    (sweet spot)
BPE (Qwen)      152,064     unhappiness              6 tokens    (bigger vocab → shorter)
Words           ~500K       unhappiness              6 tokens    (but "ChatGPT" = ???)

Each token costs compute. In Qwen-3.5 27B, every token passes through 48 transformer layers, each with attention and feed-forward operations. The rough cost: ~44 billion FLOPs per token. Fewer tokens means proportionally less work.

This is why vocabulary size matters for speed. GPT-2's 50K vocab encodes English at ~4.5 characters per token. Qwen's 152K vocab achieves ~5.2 characters per token. That 15% reduction in token count translates directly to 15% less computation, 15% less KV cache memory, 15% faster inference.

Vocabulary size × sequence length is the budget. BPE lets you tune the tradeoff.

Byte Pair Encoding

BPE is a compression algorithm repurposed for tokenization. The idea: start with characters, count all adjacent pairs, merge the most frequent pair into a single token, repeat. After a few thousand merges, common words become single tokens while rare words get split into recognizable pieces.

Worked Example
# Corpus: "low lower newest lowest"

Step 0 (characters):  l o w </w>  l o w e r </w>  n e w e s t </w>  l o w e s t </w>

Step 1  merge "l"+"o" → "lo"    (most frequent pair, appears 4 times)
        lo w </w>  lo w e r </w>  n e w e s t </w>  lo w e s t </w>

Step 2  merge "lo"+"w" → "low"
        low </w>  low e r </w>  n e w e s t </w>  low e s t </w>

Step 3  merge "e"+"s" → "es"
        low </w>  low e r </w>  n e w es t </w>  low es t </w>

Step 4  merge "es"+"t" → "est"
        low </w>  low e r </w>  n e w est </w>  low est </w>

Step 5  merge "low"+"est" → "lowest"
        low </w>  low e r </w>  n e w est </w>  lowest </w>

# After thousands of merges: common words = single tokens.
# "lowest" → 1 token.  "unhappiness" → "un" + "happiness" → 2 tokens.
# Unknown words always decompose: "defenestration" → "def" + "en" + "est" + "ration"

The key insight: BPE never fails. Any string of bytes can be tokenized because the base vocabulary includes all 256 byte values. Common patterns get compressed; rare ones fall back to characters. This is why LLMs can handle code, URLs, emoji, and text in any language.

See It Split

Notice how common words like "the" and "was" become single tokens, while the tokenizer splits rarer words into subword pieces. A larger vocabulary (like Qwen's 152K vs GPT-2's 50K) can encode more words as single tokens — fewer tokens, faster inference.

Embedding: Words as Points in Space

Tokenization gave us a sequence of integer IDs. Now each ID needs to become something a neural network can process: a vector. The embedding table is a giant lookup: token ID → a vector of 768 numbers (GPT-2) or 5,120 numbers (Qwen-3.5 27B). But why does this work? Because training pushes similar words to nearby points. The numbers aren't arbitrary — they encode meaning along learned dimensions.

Intuition
The embedding table: one row per token, 768 columns.

Token 1820 ("cat")      → [0.12, -0.34, 0.56, 0.78, -0.91, ...]  768 numbers
Token 3290 ("dog")      → [0.14, -0.31, 0.52, 0.81, -0.88, ...]  similar!
Token 9017 ("uranium")  → [-1.2, 0.89, -0.03, 0.11, 0.67, ...]  very different

"cat" and "dog" are close because training saw them in similar contexts.
"cat" and "uranium" are far because they never appear in the same patterns.

Think of it as GPS for meaning: just as (lat, lon) locates a city,
768 numbers locate a concept in meaning-space.

Python
# The entire embedding step:
x = embedding_table[token_ids]   # shape: [seq_len, 768]

# That's it. A table lookup. No math, no matrix multiply.
# embedding_table.shape = [50257, 768]   for GPT-2
#                       = [152064, 5120]  for Qwen-3.5 27B

Embedding Arithmetic

The embedding space has learned relational structure. The famous example: king − man + woman ≈ queen. This isn't a party trick — it shows that directions in the space correspond to concepts like gender, geography, and tense.

Relational Structure in Embedding Space 
  king  ────────────── queen
    │    gender          │
    │    direction       │
  man   ────────────── woman

  France ───────────── Paris
    │    capital         │
    │    direction       │
  Japan  ───────────── Tokyo

  walk  ────────────── walked
    │    tense           │
    │    direction       │
  swim  ────────────── swam
Why This Works

This arithmetic works because the embedding space is approximately linear in meaning. Directions correspond to relationships. This linearity is an emergent property of training — nobody designed it. The model learned that "king" and "queen" differ in the same way "man" and "woman" differ, because it saw them in analogous contexts billions of times.

Tied Weights: The Elegant Trick

Here's a beautiful design detail: the embedding table appears twice in the model. Once at the very beginning (token ID → vector) and once at the very end (hidden state → logits). The model is literally asking: "which token's embedding vector is closest to my output?"

Weight Tying 
  INPUT:   token 1820 ("cat") → embedding_table[1820] → [0.12, -0.34, ...]
                                       │
                          ╔═══════════════════════════╗
                          ║  48 transformer blocks...  ║
                          ╚═══════════════════════════╝
                                       │
  OUTPUT:  hidden_state → hidden_state @ embedding_table.T → logits [152,064]
                                       │
             highest score: token 318 → "is"

  Same table, used forwards and backwards.
  Saves memory. Forces input and output to share the same meaning-space.
By the Numbers

Qwen-3.5 27B: 152,064 tokens × 5,120 dimensions = 778 million parameters (~1.5 GB at FP16). That's ~5% of the model — small, but it's every word the model can read or write. The remaining 95% is the transformer blocks that process these vectors.

The text is now numbers. Each token has been looked up in the embedding table, producing a sequence of 5,120-dimensional vectors. Position information will be added via RoPE (we'll see exactly how in Chapter 3). The input is ready. Now: what does the model do with these vectors?

// 02 — The Simplest Engine

Before we optimize anything, we need to understand what we're optimizing. So let's start at the beginning — literally. February 2019. OpenAI releases GPT-2: 124 million parameters, 12 transformer layers, 768-dimensional hidden states, 12 attention heads. The world freaks out. OpenAI calls it "too dangerous to release." Today it's a toy, smaller than the model running your phone's autocomplete. But it contains every architectural idea we'll need. If you understand GPT-2's forward pass, you understand Qwen-3.5 27B's. The difference is just scale.

GPT-2 124M is small enough to hold in your head. Twelve layers. Each layer: attention (12 heads, 64 dims each) then a feed-forward network (768 → 3072 → 768). A vocabulary of 50,257 tokens. Total: 124 million floats. At FP32, that's 497 MB — fits in your laptop's RAM with room to spare. Let's write the entire forward pass.

The Complete Forward Pass

The entire model is a pipeline. Text goes in the bottom, probability distribution comes out the top. Every LLM you've ever used — Claude, GPT-4, DeepSeek, Qwen — does exactly this, with minor variations in the building blocks.


  "The capital of France"
      ↓
  ★ EMBED    Look up each token in a table of 50,257 vectors.
  │           "The" → [0.12, -0.34, 0.56, …]  (768 numbers)
  │           Add a position signal so the model knows word order.
      ↓
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ ★ NORM      Stabilise the numbers (mean = 0, variance = 1)         │
  │     ↓                                                        │
  │ ★ ATTENTION  Each token asks: "who in this sentence matters  │
  │              to me?"  Produces a relevance-weighted blend    │
  │              of all previous tokens' information.              │
  │     ↓  + skip connection (add original input back)        │
  │                                                               │
  │ ★ NORM      Stabilise again                                   │
  │     ↓                                                        │
  │ ★ FFN       Expand 768 → 3072, apply non-linearity, shrink     │
  │              back to 768.  This is where "thinking"        │
  │              happens — pattern matching, fact recall.       │
  │     ↓  + skip connection                                   │
  └───────────────────────────────────── ×12 identical ──┘
      ↓
  ★ NORM    Final stabilisation
      ↓
  ★ PROJECT  Multiply by the embedding table transposed to get
  │           a score for every word in the vocabulary.
      ↓
  [0.01, 0.02, …, 0.73, …]  ← 50,257 scores (logits)
  Highest score: token 318 = " is"

Seven matrix multiplications per block. Twelve blocks. Plus embedding + final projection. That's 86 big matmuls to produce one probability distribution. For GPT-2 that's ~250 million FLOPs. For Qwen-3.5 27B: 44 billion. Same recipe, 176× the bill.

Python
import numpy as np

def gelu(x):
    return 0.5 * x * (1 + np.tanh(np.sqrt(2/np.pi) * (x + 0.044715 * x**3)))

def softmax(x, axis=-1):
    e = np.exp(x - x.max(axis=axis, keepdims=True))
    return e / e.sum(axis=axis, keepdims=True)

def layer_norm(x, gamma, beta, eps=1e-5):
    mean = x.mean(axis=-1, keepdims=True)
    var  = x.var(axis=-1, keepdims=True)
    return gamma * (x - mean) / np.sqrt(var + eps) + beta

def attention(x, w_qkv, w_proj, b_qkv, b_proj, n_heads):
    # x: [seq_len, 768]
    B, D = x.shape
    qkv = x @ w_qkv + b_qkv          # [B, 3*768]  — project to Q, K, V
    q, k, v = np.split(qkv, 3, axis=-1)  # each [B, 768]

    # Reshape into 12 independent heads of dim 64
    head_dim = D // n_heads            # 768/12 = 64
    q = q.reshape(B, n_heads, head_dim).transpose(1,0,2)
    k = k.reshape(B, n_heads, head_dim).transpose(1,0,2)
    v = v.reshape(B, n_heads, head_dim).transpose(1,0,2)

    # Causal attention: score every pair, mask the future, softmax, blend
    scores = (q @ k.transpose(0,2,1)) / np.sqrt(head_dim)  # [12, B, B]
    mask = np.triu(np.full((B, B), -1e9), k=1)   # -inf above diagonal
    scores += mask
    attn = softmax(scores)             # attention weights
    out = (attn @ v)                   # [12, B, 64]  weighted blend of values

    # Merge heads back to [B, 768] and project
    out = out.transpose(1,0,2).reshape(B, D)
    return out @ w_proj + b_proj

def ffn(x, w_fc, b_fc, w_proj, b_proj):
    h = gelu(x @ w_fc + b_fc)         # [B, 768] → [B, 3072]  expand + activate
    return h @ w_proj + b_proj         # [B, 3072] → [B, 768]  compress back

def transformer_block(x, block):
    # Pre-norm → attention → residual
    h = layer_norm(x, block['ln1_g'], block['ln1_b'])
    x = x + attention(h, block['attn_qkv_w'], block['attn_proj_w'],
                          block['attn_qkv_b'], block['attn_proj_b'], 12)
    # Pre-norm → FFN → residual
    h = layer_norm(x, block['ln2_g'], block['ln2_b'])
    x = x + ffn(h, block['fc_w'], block['fc_b'],
                   block['proj_w'], block['proj_b'])
    return x

def gpt2_forward(token_ids, model):
    x = model['wte'][token_ids]  +  model['wpe'][np.arange(len(token_ids))]
    # wte: [50257, 768] token embeddings
    # wpe: [1024, 768]  position embeddings (max 1024 context)

    for block in model['blocks']:       # 12 transformer blocks
        x = transformer_block(x, block)

    x = layer_norm(x, model['ln_f_g'], model['ln_f_b'])
    logits = x @ model['wte'].T       # tie weights: reuse embedding table
    return logits                       # [seq_len, 50257]

Every LLM does exactly this. RMSNorm instead of LayerNorm. SwiGLU instead of GELU. RoPE instead of learned positions. The skeleton is the same. The variations in Chapter 3.

Hybrid Attention Note

Qwen-3.5 introduces hybrid attention: ~75% of layers use Gated DeltaNet (a linear attention variant for efficient long-range processing), while ~25% use full GQA (standard softmax attention for precise retrieval). We'll focus on the GQA layers throughout this deep dive — they contain all the mechanisms that matter for understanding inference. The DeltaNet layers are a compute optimization that avoids the quadratic attention cost on most layers.

The Autoregressive Loop

A language model generates text one token at a time. Run the forward pass, look at the last position's logits, sample a token, append it, repeat. Hit play to watch it happen.

This is the entire generation loop. The visualization above is running this logic.

Python
def generate(prompt_tokens, model, max_tokens=50, temperature=0.8):
    tokens = list(prompt_tokens)
    for _ in range(max_tokens):
        logits = gpt2_forward(tokens, model)  # run ALL tokens through
        next_logits = logits[-1]               # only last position matters
        probs = softmax(next_logits / temperature)
        next_token = np.random.choice(len(probs), p=probs)
        tokens.append(next_token)
        if next_token == eos_token: break
    return tokens

# "The capital of France" → " is Paris, a city known for..."

Notice the waste: at step 50 we rerun all 50 tokens through all 12 layers — even though tokens 1-49 haven't changed. That's 98% redundant work. The KV cache (Chapter 7) fixes this.

At each step: 12 layers × 7 matmuls = 84 matrix multiplications, plus attention softmax, GELU, and norms. For GPT-2 that's ~250M FLOPs per token. For Qwen-3.5 27B it's 44 billion. Same architecture, 176× the cost.

The Anchor

This 60-line forward pass IS a working inference engine. Everything in the remaining chapters is "how do we make this faster, bigger, and serve it to thousands of users." Quantization makes the weights smaller. KV caching avoids recomputation. FlashAttention does the attention in fast SRAM. Batching shares the weight reads. But the core loop — embed, attend, project, sample, repeat — never changes.

GPT-2 was 124 million parameters. Today's frontier models are 27 billion (Qwen-3.5 27B), 671 billion (DeepSeek-V3, a Mixture-of-Experts model), or entirely undisclosed (Claude Opus 4.5). The dense architecture is the same. The scale is not. Qwen-3.5 27B: 64 layers, 5120-dimensional hidden state, 24 query heads, 4 KV heads (GQA), 17408-wide FFN. The weight file is 54 GB in FP16. That's too large for a single consumer GPU, but quantize it to 4-bit (~15 GB) and it fits on your RTX 3090 with room to spare. The question is: how do you make those numbers fast?

In Chapter 2 we wrote the forward pass for GPT-2 in 60 lines. Now let's trace a single vector through one transformer block at instruction level — the way the hardware sees it. We'll use concrete numbers, not abstractions. One vector, one block, every operation.

Worked Example: One Token Through One Block

Our input is a vector x of shape [768] for GPT-2, or [5120] for Qwen-3.5 27B. Let's trace through with real (small) numbers to build intuition. Imagine x = [0.5, -1.2, 0.8, 0.3, ...] — a 768-dimensional vector representing the current token's hidden state.

One Transformer Block — Data Flow 

  input x [5120]
      │
  ① RMSNorm  ··· scale by learned γ, no bias
      │
      ├→ W_q [5120×6144] → Q [24 heads × 256]  ②
      ├→ W_k [5120×1024] → K [4 heads × 256]   ② QKV Projection
      ├→ W_v [5120×1024] → V [4 heads × 256]   ②
      │
      │   ↓ apply RoPE to Q, K (position encoding) ③
      │
  ④ Attention   scores = softmax(Q · KT / √d)
      │               output = scores · V
      │               ↓
      ├→ W_out [6144×5120] → projection back to model dim
      │
  ⊕ residual   add original x back  ← skip connection
      │
  RMSNorm  ··· normalize again
      │
      ├→ W_gate [5120×17408] → gate  ⑤
      │                              │
      │       SiLU(gate) ⊙ up ←────────⑤ SwiGLU FFN
      │                              │
      ├→ W_up   [5120×17408] → up   ⑤
      │
      ├→ W_down [17408×5120] → back to [5120]
      │
  ⊕ residual   add pre-FFN input back  ← skip connection
      │
  output → next block (or final norm + logits)

  7 matmuls per block: W_q, W_k, W_v, W_out, W_gate, W_up, W_down
  × 64 blocks = 448 matmuls per token

Step 1: RMSNorm

Modern models use RMSNorm instead of LayerNorm (GPT-2 used LayerNorm). The difference: RMSNorm skips the mean-centering step and has no bias. It's simpler and empirically works just as well.

RMSNorm — why normalize at all? 

  Problem: after each layer, vector magnitudes drift.
  Some dimensions explode, others shrink. Without correction,
  later layers see wildly different scales → training collapses.

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Before RMSNorm:

    dim 0:  ████████████████████  340.2    ← exploding
    dim 1:  ██                      0.003    ← vanishing
    dim 2:  ████████████            89.7

  After RMSNorm:

    dim 0:  ████                     1.73     ✓ tamed
    dim 1:  █                        0.00002  ✓ ratio preserved
    dim 2:  ██                       0.46     ✓ tamed

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Divide every dimension by the same number (the RMS).
  Relative proportions stay intact. Absolute scale is reset.
  Then γ (learned per-dimension) re-scales what matters.

  vs LayerNorm: RMSNorm skips mean-centering (no bias shift).
  Simpler, faster, works just as well — that’s why every modern LLM uses it.

Math
# Input: x = [0.5, -1.2, 0.8, 0.3, ...] (768 values)

RMS(x) = sqrt(mean(x²))
       = sqrt( (0.25 + 1.44 + 0.64 + 0.09 + ...) / 768 )
       = sqrt(0.847)    # typical RMS for a normalized hidden state
       = 0.920

x_norm = x / RMS(x)
       = [0.543, -1.304, 0.870, 0.326, ...]

output = x_norm * gamma   # gamma is a learned per-dimension scale [768]
       = [0.543 * 1.02, -1.304 * 0.97, ...]
       = [0.554, -1.265, ...]

# Cost: 3 passes over the vector (square, sum, divide+multiply)
# FLOPs: ~3 × 768 = 2,304. Trivial compared to matmul.

Step 2: QKV Projection

This is where the real cost starts. We multiply our normalized vector by three large weight matrices to get queries, keys, and values:

Math
# For Qwen-3.5 27B with GQA (4 KV heads, 24 query heads, head_dim=256):

Q = x_norm @ W_q     # [5120] × [5120, 6144] → [6144]  (24 heads × 256 dim)
K = x_norm @ W_k     # [5120] × [5120, 1024] → [1024]  (4 heads × 256 dim)
V = x_norm @ W_v     # [5120] × [5120, 1024] → [1024]  (4 heads × 256 dim)

# FLOPs for Q projection alone:
#   5120 × 6144 × 2 = 62,914,560 (63M FLOPs)
# K and V: 5120 × 1024 × 2 × 2 = 20.9M FLOPs
# Total QKV: ~84M FLOPs. Per block. Per token.

Notice the asymmetry: Q is 6× larger than K or V because of GQA. There are 24 query heads but only 4 KV heads — each KV head is shared across 6 query heads. Also note that W_q is not square: the output (6144) is larger than the input (5120) because head_dim (256) × n_heads (24) ≠ d_model (5120).

Q, K, V — what they actually mean 

  Every token produces three vectors from the same input:

  Q = "What am I looking for?"        the question this token asks
  K = "What do I contain?"             the label this token advertises
  V = "What information do I carry?"   the payload delivered if selected

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Example: token "France" at position 4

  QFrance ≈ "I need context about countries, geography, politics"
  KFrance ≈ "I am a country name, European, noun"
  VFrance ≈ [rich vector encoding France's learned features]

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  The attention mechanism in two steps:

  Attention weights = softmax(Q · KT)
      ↑ match every query against every key
      ↑ high dot product = "this key answers my query"

  Output = Attention · V
      ↑ read the winning payloads, weighted by relevance

  Key insight: K decides WHO gets attention. V decides WHAT flows.
  They're decoupled — a token can be highly relevant (strong K match)
  but carry different information (V) than what K advertises.
Grouped-Query Attention — 64 query heads, 8 KV heads 

  Query heads (24)                          KV heads (4)

  Q0  Q1  Q2  Q3  Q4  Q5   →→→  KV0    group 0
  Q6  Q7  Q8  Q9  Q10 Q11  →→→  KV1    group 1
  Q12 Q13 Q14 Q15 Q16 Q17 →→→  KV2    group 2
  Q18 Q19 Q20 Q21 Q22 Q23 →→→  KV3    group 3

  Each group: 6 query heads share 1 KV head
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                Multi-Head      Grouped-Query      Multi-Query
                Attention        Attention          Attention
    KV heads:     24               4                  1
    KV cache:   100%             16.7%              4.2%
    Quality:    baseline         ≈ same             slightly worse

  6 queries share 1 KV pair → 6× less KV cache memory
  At 4K context: 1 GB KV cache instead of 6 GB
Dimension flow through QKV projection 

  Input:   x_norm [5120]

  Step 1: project into Q, K, V

    x_norm [5120] × W_q [5120 × 6144] → Q [6144]
    x_norm [5120] × W_k [5120 × 1024] → K [1024]
    x_norm [5120] × W_v [5120 × 1024] → V [1024]

  Step 2: reshape into heads

    Q [6144] → [24 heads × 256 dim]   (24 query heads)
    K [1024] → [4 heads × 256 dim]    (4 KV heads)
    V [1024] → [4 heads × 256 dim]    (4 KV heads)

  Step 3: attention (per head group)

    scores = Qhead [1 × 256] · KcacheT [256 × seq_len] → [1 × seq_len]
    weights = softmax(scores / √256) → [1 × seq_len]
    out = weights [1 × seq_len] · Vcache [seq_len × 256] → [1 × 256]

  Step 4: concatenate all 24 heads, project back

    concat [24 × 256] = [6144] × W_out [6144 × 5120] → [5120]

Step 3: RoPE (Position Encoding)

How does the model know that "Paris" is the fifth word, not the first? Rotary Position Embedding encodes position by rotating query and key vectors in pairs of dimensions. Each dimension pair rotates at a different frequency, creating a unique angular signature for each position.

RoPE — position as rotation 

  Each dimension pair is a clock hand spinning at a different speed.
  Position = how far each hand has rotated from the start.

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Dimension pair i=0 (slowest clock):

    pos 0:  ↑  0°       pos 1:  ↗  0.06°    pos 5:  ↗  0.3°
            tiny rotation per step → detects long-range gaps

  Dimension pair i=32 (medium clock):

    pos 0:  ↑  0°       pos 1:  →  5.6°     pos 5:  ↓  28°
            moderate rotation → mid-range position sensitivity

  Dimension pair i=63 (fastest clock):

    pos 0:  ↑  0°       pos 1:  ↙  180°    pos 5:  ↙  900°
            rapid rotation → distinguishes adjacent tokens

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Each position gets a unique angular “fingerprint” across all 64 pairs.
  No two positions produce the same combination of angles.

Python
# RoPE: rotate pairs of dimensions by position-dependent angles
# For dimension pair (2i, 2i+1) at position pos:

freq = 1.0 / (base ** (2*i / d_model))  # base=10000000 for Qwen-3.5
theta = pos * freq

# Low frequency pairs (i=0): rotate slowly → captures long-range position
# High frequency pairs (i=63): rotate fast → captures fine position

q[2*i]   = q_orig[2*i]   * cos(theta) - q_orig[2*i+1] * sin(theta)
q[2*i+1] = q_orig[2*i]   * sin(theta) + q_orig[2*i+1] * cos(theta)

# The key insight: Q·K^T now encodes RELATIVE position.
# "How far apart are these tokens?" — not "what absolute position?"
# This is why context extension (YaRN, NTK) works: adjust frequencies.
Position invariance — only the gap matters 

  Scenario A:  Q at position 5,  K at position 3   → gap = 2
  Scenario B:  Q at position 100, K at position 98  → gap = 2

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  How RoPE makes this work:

  Q·KT after rotation = f(pos_Q − pos_K)

  The rotation of Q at pos 5 minus the rotation of K at pos 3
  = the same angle as Q at pos 100 minus K at pos 98.

  R(5) · R(3)T  =  R(5−3)  =  R(2)
  R(100) · R(98)T =  R(100−98) =  R(2)  ✓ identical

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Rotation matrices cancel: R(θ) · R(φ)T = R(θ−φ)
  This is why context extension (YaRN, NTK) works:
  rescale the frequencies → stretch the position space.

Soft Lookup Table

Attention is a soft lookup table. Q is the search query, K is the index, V is the data. Unlike a hash table, every entry matches to some degree — softmax just decides how much.

Step 5: SwiGLU FFN

After attention blends information across tokens, the FFN processes each token independently. This is where the model actually "thinks" — pattern matching, fact recall, reasoning. It's the largest single compute cost per block, and modern models use a gated variant called SwiGLU that's fundamentally different from GPT-2's simple approach.

The problem SwiGLU solves. GPT-2's FFN was simple: expand the vector to 4× width, apply GELU activation, shrink back. Two matrices, one non-linearity. The activation decided which dimensions to keep — but it had to make that decision using only the expanded representation itself. There was no separate "should I use this?" signal.

SwiGLU splits the decision from the content. Two parallel projections from the same input:


  Same input x feeds both paths:

  GATE path   x → W_gate → SiLU → "should this feature activate?"
                                     high → open the gate
                                     low  → shut it down

  UP path     x → W_up →          "what value should it carry?"
                                     the candidate content

  COMBINE     SiLU(gate) ⊙ up          element-wise multiply
                                     gate decides, up provides

  DOWN        result → W_down →       compress back to [5120]

The gate learns which features are relevant for this token. The up path computes candidate values for every feature. Multiplying them is selective routing: if the gate says "this feature doesn't matter here," it drives the output toward zero — regardless of what up computed.

Think of it like a mixing board. Each of the 17,408 channels has a fader (the gate) and an audio signal (the up value). The model learned which faders to push up for each kind of input. A token about geography might open channels for location features and close channels for emotion features. A token about sentiment does the opposite. Same weights, different routing per token.

The cost: three weight matrices instead of two. That's why the FFN dominates compute — 535M FLOPs per block, ~78% of the per-block budget. But the quality improvement is consistent and significant, which is why every post-2022 model uses it.

SwiGLU (4 lines)
gate = x @ W_gate    # [5120] → [17408]  "should this feature activate?"
up   = x @ W_up      # [5120] → [17408]  "what value should it carry?"
h    = SiLU(gate) ⊙ up   # element-wise: gate decides, up provides
out  = h @ W_down     # [17408] → [5120]  compress back

# vs GPT-2's FFN (2 matrices, no gate):
# h = GELU(x @ W_fc)     # expand + activate
# out = h @ W_proj        # compress

# FLOPs: 3 × 5120 × 17408 × 2 = 535M per block per token
# Attention FLOPs: ~147M. FFN is ~78% of per-block compute.

SiLU (Sigmoid Linear Unit): SiLU(x) = x × sigmoid(x)


  How SiLU behaves at different gate values:

  gate = -3.0  →  sigmoid = 0.05  →  SiLU = -0.14    nearly killed
  gate = -1.0  →  sigmoid = 0.27  →  SiLU = -0.27    suppressed
  gate =  0.0  →  sigmoid = 0.50  →  SiLU =  0.00    on the fence
  gate =  1.0  →  sigmoid = 0.73  →  SiLU =  0.73    mostly open
  gate =  3.0  →  sigmoid = 0.95  →  SiLU =  2.86    wide open

  vs ReLU: hard cutoff at 0. Everything negative → dead zero.
  vs SiLU: smooth transition. Gradients flow even for negative inputs.
  This smoothness gives the model fine-grained control over
  "how open" each gate is — not just on/off.

SiLU has a unique property: it can output small negative values (minimum around -0.28 at x ≈ -1.28). This means the gate can slightly invert a feature, not just suppress it. ReLU can't do this. GELU is similar but lacks the clean multiplicative gating structure that makes SwiGLU work.

Why Two Projections?

The gate learns which features matter. The up-projection computes candidate values. Multiplying them = selective routing. It's like an if-statement learned from data: if gate[i] > 0: output[i] = up[i], but smooth and differentiable. One projection decides, the other provides — that's why SwiGLU needs three matrices instead of GPT-2's two, and why it works better: the model can learn to shut off irrelevant features instead of blending everything.

The Machine Understood

We now know what the model is: 64 repetitions of norm → attention → norm → FFN, with residual connections. We know what each weight matrix does and its exact dimensions. Seven matrix multiplications per block. Sixty-four blocks. 44 billion FLOPs per token. For every single token the model generates, all of this runs. Every matrix. Every layer. Every time.

We can write the forward pass. We know the math. But the forward pass only produces a vector of raw scores — logits. How do you turn those into an actual word? That's the sampling pipeline, and it's the final step that completes the picture: weights → forward pass → logits → token. Let's close that loop.

// 04 — Choosing Words

The model's forward pass produces a vector of logits — one raw score for every token in the vocabulary (32,000 to 128,000 values). Converting this into a single chosen token is the sampling pipeline's job. Temperature, top-p, top-k, min-p, repetition penalty — each is a filter applied to the logit distribution before sampling, and their order matters.

The Pipeline

llama.cpp default order
1. Repetition Penalty  — reduce recently-used tokens
2. Top-K              — keep only K highest-scoring tokens
3. Top-P (nucleus)    — keep smallest set with cumulative prob ≥ p
4. Min-P              — remove tokens below min_p × max_probability
5. Temperature        — scale logits: logits /= T
6. Softmax            — convert to probabilities
7. Random Sample      — draw one token from the distribution

Temperature: Worked Example

Same logits, different temperatures
# Raw logits for top 5 tokens: [5.2, 3.8, 3.1, 2.4, 1.7]

T=0.3 (very focused):
  logits/T = [17.3, 12.7, 10.3, 8.0, 5.7]
  softmax  = [0.990, 0.007, 0.001, 0.000, 0.000]
  → Almost deterministic. Top token wins 99% of the time.

T=0.7 (balanced):
  logits/T = [7.4, 5.4, 4.4, 3.4, 2.4]

  logits/T = [3.5, 2.5, 2.1, 1.6, 1.1]

Act II
Why It's Hard
You understand the machine. Now let's find out why running it is the real challenge.

// 05 — The Numbers in the File

It starts with a download. You point your browser at Hugging Face, pick a model — say, Qwen-3.5 27B — and wait. What arrives is not a program. It is a file of numbers — twenty-seven billion of them, each a 16-bit floating-point value, arranged in matrices that were painstakingly tuned over months of training on thousands of GPUs. The file is 54 gigabytes. It contains no instructions, no logic, no if-statements. Just weights.

Walking Through the Bytes

Let's look at what's actually on disk. The two dominant formats organize the data differently, but both follow the same principle: a header describing the model's architecture, followed by raw tensor data.

The Design Insight

Self-contained + aligned = instant load. GGUF embeds the tokenizer, architecture params, and weights in one file. The tensor data starts at a 16-byte-aligned offset. This means mmap() can map the file directly — the OS's virtual memory system becomes your model loader. A 15 GB model "loads" in under a second because nothing is read until computation touches a page.

Safetensors (Hugging Face) is a multi-file format used by Python-based engines like vLLM. An 8-byte length prefix, then a JSON header describing tensor names/shapes/offsets, then raw tensor data. The tokenizer lives in separate files, and the weights are typically sharded across multiple files (model-00001-of-00004.safetensors) for parallel loading.

The Growth

The FFN dominates. In Qwen-3.5 27B, the feed-forward network is ~60% of all parameters. Attention is ~33%. Embeddings are ~5% (larger vocabulary). This is why the FFN is the primary target for quantization — it's where the bytes are.

The First Problem

54 gigabytes at FP16. Your RTX 3090 has 24 GB of VRAM. It doesn't fit at full precision. But quantize to 4-bit (~15 GB) and it fits with room for a KV cache. Larger models like 72B still don't fit at all.

We have the weights, we know their shapes, we know they don't fit at full precision. But what happens when we actually run inference? The answer depends on which phase of inference you're in — and they're nothing alike.
