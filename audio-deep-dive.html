01 — What Is Sound?

Pressure waves ripple through the air like invisible rings on a pond. When something vibrates — a guitar string, a speaker cone, your vocal cords — it pushes and pulls on the surrounding air molecules, creating alternating zones of high and low pressure that radiate outward at roughly 343 meters per second. That is sound. Everything you have ever heard, from a thunderclap to a whisper, is nothing more than air molecules jostling their neighbours in a chain reaction that ends at your eardrum.

Frequency determines pitch: a low frequency like 80 Hz sounds like a bass rumble, while 4,000 Hz is a sharp whistle. Amplitude determines volume: bigger pressure swings mean louder sound. These two properties — how fast the wave oscillates and how far it swings — encode almost everything about a tone.

Human Hearing Range

The human ear responds to frequencies between roughly 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz (20 kHz). Below 20 Hz is infrasound — you might feel it in your chest at a concert but you cannot hear it. Above 20 kHz is ultrasound — the domain of bats and medical imaging. Most adults lose the top end with age; by 30, you are likely deaf above 16 kHz.

It Is All Sine Waves

Fourier proved in 1822 that any periodic waveform — no matter how jagged — can be decomposed into a sum of sine waves at different frequencies and amplitudes. This is the single most important idea in audio. Every synthesizer, every codec, every equalizer rests on Fourier's insight: complex sounds are just stacks of simple sines.

02 — Sampling: Catching a Wave

Analog sound is continuous — an infinite river of pressure values flowing through time. To store it in a computer, we must freeze-frame that river at regular intervals. Each snapshot is a sample: a single number representing the pressure at that instant. String enough samples together — 44,100 per second for CD audio — and you can reconstruct the original wave with startling fidelity. This analog-to-digital conversion is where the physical world becomes math.

The Nyquist Theorem

Harry Nyquist and Claude Shannon showed that to perfectly capture a frequency f, you must sample at at least 2f. This is the Nyquist frequency. CD audio samples at 44,100 Hz, which perfectly captures everything up to 22,050 Hz — comfortably above human hearing. Sample too slowly, and high frequencies fold back as phantom tones called aliasing — a siren that sounds like a wobble, a cymbal that sounds like static.

Bit Depth

Each sample is quantized to a fixed number of bits. More bits means finer resolution and a higher dynamic range (the span between the quietest and loudest representable signals).

The Nyquist Wall

If you generate a 20 kHz tone and sample it at 40 kHz, you get the theoretical minimum — two samples per cycle. The result is a jagged zigzag that barely hints at a sine. One sample fewer per second and the signal aliases: a 20 kHz tone sampled at 39,999 Hz becomes a phantom 19,999 Hz tone. There is no recovering the original. The Nyquist wall is absolute — cross it and information is destroyed.

03 — Oscillators: The Atoms of Sound

An oscillator is a function that produces a repeating waveform — the atomic building block of every synthesizer. Feed it a frequency and it spits out a stream of samples tracing the same shape over and over. Everything from an 808 kick to a lush pad starts with one or more oscillators running in parallel, their outputs summed and sculpted into something musical.

The Four Classic Waveforms

Virtually every synthesizer offers these four shapes. They differ in harmonic content — which overtones ride on top of the fundamental frequency.

Classic Waveform Shapes 
 SINE          SQUARE        SAWTOOTH      TRIANGLE
    *              ┌──┐           /|          /\
  *   *         │  │        / |        /  \
 *     *        │  │       /  |       /    \
*       *  ────│  │──── /   | ──── /      \
         *      │     │  /    |     /        \
          *  *  │     └──  /     |    /          \
           *               /      |

Sine — a single pure frequency, no harmonics. The fundamental tone. Square — odd harmonics only (3rd, 5th, 7th...), each at 1/n amplitude. Hollow, woody sound. Sawtooth — all harmonics (odd and even) at 1/n amplitude. Bright, buzzy, the richest of the four. Triangle — odd harmonics only at 1/n² amplitude. Softer than square, almost sine-like.

Square Waves Have Infinite Bandwidth

A perfect square wave requires an infinite sum of odd harmonics. In the real world, this is impossible — every physical system has a bandwidth limit. The sharp corners of a theoretical square wave contain energy at arbitrarily high frequencies. This is why naive digital square waves alias so badly: those infinite harmonics fold back below Nyquist as inharmonic garbage. Band-limited synthesis techniques like PolyBLEP exist specifically to tame this problem.

04 — Additive and Subtractive Synthesis

Two opposing philosophies dominate the history of synthesis. Additive synthesis builds complex sounds from the ground up by stacking simple sine waves — like painting with individual brushstrokes of frequency. Subtractive synthesis starts with a harmonically rich source (a sawtooth or square oscillator) and carves away frequencies with filters — like sculpting a block of marble. Both arrive at the same destination through opposite routes.

Additive Synthesis

The idea is pure Fourier: any sound can be constructed by summing sinusoids at the right frequencies, amplitudes, and phases. An organ pipe, a clarinet, a human vowel — each is just a specific recipe of partials. The catch is cost: modeling a rich sound might require hundreds of oscillators, each with its own envelope. Early additive synths (the Fairlight CMI, Kawai K5) were expensive precisely because of this computational weight.

Subtractive Synthesis

Start with a waveform that already contains all the harmonics you need — a sawtooth is the classic choice — then use filters to remove what you do not want. This is the architecture of the Moog, the Roland Juno, the SH-101, and nearly every analog synth ever made. It is computationally cheap, musically intuitive, and the reason "filter sweep" is a fundamental sound design technique.

05 — Filters: Sculpting Frequency

Filters are the sculptor's chisel of sound design. They selectively attenuate frequencies, transforming a raw oscillator tone into something musical. A low-pass filter makes a bright sawtooth warm and mellow. A high-pass filter strips out mud and rumble. A bandpass isolates a narrow frequency band, like tuning a radio dial. Understanding filters is understanding the difference between a synth preset that sounds amateur and one that sounds professional.

Filter Types

Low-pass (LP) — passes frequencies below the cutoff, attenuates above. The most common filter in synthesis. High-pass (HP) — the opposite: passes above, cuts below. Used to remove bass rumble or thin out a sound. Band-pass (BP) — passes a narrow band around the cutoff, attenuates both sides. Think wah-wah pedal. Notch (Band-reject) — the inverse of band-pass: cuts a narrow band, passes everything else. Used to remove specific resonances.

Cutoff, Resonance, and Slope

The cutoff frequency is where the filter begins attenuating. Resonance (Q) boosts frequencies near the cutoff, creating a peak that makes the filter "sing." Push Q high enough and the filter self-oscillates — it becomes an oscillator itself. Slope (measured in dB/octave) determines how aggressively the filter rolls off: a 12 dB/oct filter is gentle, a 24 dB/oct filter is steep, and a 48 dB/oct filter is a brick wall.

typedef struct {

void biquad_lowpass(Biquad *bq, double sr, double cutoff, double Q) {
    double w0 = 2.0 * M_PI * cutoff / sr;
    double alpha = sin(w0) / (2.0 * Q);
    double cosw0 = cos(w0);
    double a0 = 1.0 + alpha;

    bq->b0 = ((1.0 - cosw0) / 2.0) / a0;
    bq->b1 = (1.0 - cosw0) / a0;
    bq->b2 = bq->b0;
    bq->a1 = (-2.0 * cosw0) / a0;
    bq->a2 = (1.0 - alpha) / a0;
    bq->x1 = bq->x2 = bq->y1 = bq->y2 = 0.0;
}

double biquad_process(Biquad *bq, double x0) {
    double y0 = bq->b0 * x0 + bq->b1 * bq->x1 + bq->b2 * bq->x2
              - bq->a1 * bq->y1 - bq->a2 * bq->y2;
    bq->x2 = bq->x1; bq->x1 = x0;
    bq->y2 = bq->y1; bq->y1 = y0;
    return y0;
}

Resonance Is Feedback

Resonance works by feeding the filter's output back into its input at the cutoff frequency. This positive feedback loop amplifies a narrow band until, at maximum Q, the filter self-oscillates — producing a pure sine wave at the cutoff frequency with no input signal at all. The Moog ladder filter's distinctive scream is literally the sound of controlled feedback. Push it too far and you get runaway gain — which is why every real filter needs a saturation stage to keep the math (and your speakers) from exploding.

06 — Envelopes: The Shape of a Note

Notes do not just switch on and off. A piano key struck hard has a bright, percussive attack that quickly mellows. A violin note swells slowly, sustains indefinitely while the bow moves, then fades when it lifts. An envelope describes how a parameter — usually amplitude, but also filter cutoff or pitch — changes over the lifetime of a note. Without envelopes, every sound would be an organ: on or off, nothing in between.

ADSR — The Universal Envelope

The ADSR model splits a note's life into four stages:

Attack — how long it takes to rise from zero to full level after a key is pressed. A snare drum has an attack near 0 ms; a slow pad might take 2 seconds. Decay — how long it takes to fall from the peak to the sustain level. A plucked string has a fast decay. Sustain — the steady-state level held as long as the key is held. This is a level, not a time. A pipe organ sustains at 100%; a xylophone sustains at 0%. Release — how long it takes to fade to zero after the key is released. A bell has a long release; a muted guitar has almost none.

Ctypedef enum { ENV_IDLE, ENV_ATTACK, ENV_DECAY, ENV_SUSTAIN, ENV_RELEASE } EnvStage;

typedef struct {
    double attack, decay, sustain, release; // seconds / level
    double level;
    double sr;
    EnvStage stage;
} ADSR;

void adsr_init(ADSR *e, double sr, double a, double d, double s, double r) {
    e->sr = sr; e->attack = a; e->decay = d;
    e->sustain = s; e->release = r;
    e->level = 0.0; e->stage = ENV_IDLE;
}

void adsr_gate(ADSR *e, int on) {
    e->stage = on ? ENV_ATTACK : ENV_RELEASE;
}

double adsr_process(ADSR *e) {
    switch (e->stage) {
        case ENV_ATTACK:
            e->level += 1.0 / (e->attack * e->sr);
            if (e->level >= 1.0) { e->level = 1.0; e->stage = ENV_DECAY; }
            break;
        case ENV_DECAY:
            e->level -= (1.0 - e->sustain) / (e->decay * e->sr);
            if (e->level <= e->sustain) { e->level = e->sustain; e->stage = ENV_SUSTAIN; }
            break;
        case ENV_SUSTAIN:
            break; // hold at sustain level
        case ENV_RELEASE:
            e->level -= e->sustain / (e->release * e->sr);
            if (e->level <= 0.0) { e->level = 0.0; e->stage = ENV_IDLE; }
            break;
        case ENV_IDLE:
            break;
    }
    return e->level;
}

    # Decay: ramp 1 → sustain
    d = np.linspace(1, sustain, d_samples) if d_samples > 0 else np.array([])
    # Sustain: hold at sustain level
    s = np.full(h_samples, sustain)
    # Release: ramp sustain → 0
    r = np.linspace(sustain, 0, r_samples) if r_samples > 0 else np.array([])

    return np.concatenate([a, d, s, r])

07 Effects: Delay, Reverb, Distortion

Every sound you hear in recorded music has been processed through a chain of effects. The raw signal from a microphone or synthesizer is just the starting point — what reaches your ears has been delayed, reverberated, filtered, compressed, and shaped by algorithms that are themselves fascinating studies in signal processing. Effects are where math becomes art.

Delay Lines: The Circular Buffer

A delay effect stores audio samples in a buffer and plays them back after a fixed time. The core data structure is a circular buffer (ring buffer) — you write samples at the head, read them from a position behind the head, and when you hit the end of the buffer you wrap around to the beginning.

Delay Line with Feedback 

  Input ──►[+]──────────────► Output
             ▲                   │
             │                   │ wet/dry mix
             │    ┌──────────┐   │
             └────┤ DELAY    │◄──┘
              fb  │ (ring    │  feedback
              gain│ buffer)  │  gain
                  └──────────┘

  Ring Buffer:
  ┌───┬───┬───┬───┬───┬───┬───┬───┐
  │ . │ . │ R │ . │ . │ . │ W │ . │
  └───┴───┴───┴───┴───┴───┴───┴───┘
              ▲               ▲
              read            write
              (delay_ms       (current
               behind)        sample)

The feedback parameter controls how much of the delayed signal feeds back into the input. At 0% you get a single echo. At 50% you get decaying repeats. At 100% you get infinite repeats (and eventual clipping). The wet/dry mix blends the original signal with the delayed signal.

Reverb: Convolution with Space

Reverb simulates the reflections of sound in a physical space. There are two fundamental approaches:

Convolution reverb captures the acoustic signature of a real space as an impulse response (IR) — record a short, sharp sound (like a clap or starter pistol) in a cathedral, and the recording captures every reflection, absorption, and diffusion pattern of that space. To apply this reverb, you convolve the dry audio with the IR. Mathematically this is multiplication in the frequency domain: FFT both signals, multiply, inverse FFT.

Algorithmic reverb builds the effect from chains of delay lines and filters. The classic designs:

  • Schroeder reverb (1962): parallel comb filters feeding into series allpass filters. Simple but metallic-sounding.
  • Freeverb (Jezar, 2000): 8 comb filters + 4 allpass filters with carefully tuned delay lengths. Still used everywhere because it sounds good and is cheap to compute.
  • Feedback Delay Networks (FDN): generalization where N delay lines are mixed through a unitary matrix. Modern algorithmic reverbs are mostly FDNs.

Distortion: Waveshaping

Distortion applies a nonlinear transfer function to the signal. The input sample value maps to a new output value through a curve:

  • Hard clipping: out = clamp(in, -threshold, threshold). Brutal, digital-sounding.
  • Soft clipping: out = tanh(gain * in). Smooth saturation, tube-amp character.
  • Overdrive: asymmetric clipping where positive and negative halves are shaped differently. Mimics transistor circuits.
  • Bitcrusher: reduce bit depth by quantizing. out = round(in * levels) / levels.

The key insight: distortion creates harmonics. A pure sine wave through a symmetric clipper gains odd harmonics (3rd, 5th, 7th...). Asymmetric clipping adds even harmonics too. This is why tube amps sound "warm" — they add primarily even harmonics from their asymmetric saturation curve.

Reverb Is Convolution

Convolution reverb is the same mathematical operation used in image processing, neural networks, and signal filtering. When you apply a reverb IR to audio, you are literally computing y[n] = SUM(x[k] * h[n-k]) — the same convolution kernel operation a CNN uses on pixels. The only difference is dimensionality: audio is 1D, images are 2D, video is 3D. The FFT trick that makes convolution reverb practical (O(N log N) instead of O(N²)) is the same trick that accelerates any large convolution. Every reverb plugin is secretly a neural network layer running in the frequency domain.

08 FM Synthesis: The DX7 Revolution

In 1967, John Chowning was experimenting with extreme vibrato at Stanford's Center for Computer Research in Music and Acoustics. He pushed the modulation frequency above 20 Hz — past the point where you hear individual pitch wobbles — and something remarkable happened. The timbre itself changed. New harmonics appeared from nowhere. He had accidentally discovered frequency modulation synthesis, a technique that would spawn the best-selling synthesizer in history and define the sound of 1980s pop music.

FM = Modulating Frequency with Frequency

The basic FM equation for a two-operator system:

Two-Operator FM Synthesis 

  output(t) = sin(2π * fc * t  +  d * sin(2π * fm * t))

  Where:
    fc = carrier frequency       (determines pitch)
    fm = modulator frequency     (determines harmonic structure)
    d  = modulation depth/index  (determines brightness)

  Ratio = fm / fc
    Integer ratios  → harmonic spectra (musical tones)
    Non-integer     → inharmonic spectra (bells, metallic sounds)

  Modulation Index = d / fm
    Low index  (0-1)   → few sidebands, simple tone
    High index (5-10+) → many sidebands, complex/bright tone

The DX7: 6 Operators, 32 Algorithms

Yamaha licensed Chowning's patent and released the DX7 in 1983. Instead of two operators, the DX7 has six, each a sine oscillator with its own envelope. The 32 algorithms are fixed routings of these operators — which modulates which, which goes directly to the output (carriers), and which feeds back into itself. Algorithm 1 is a simple stack (6→5→4→3→2→1). Algorithm 32 puts all six in parallel as carriers. The space between these extremes is where the DX7's extraordinary timbral range lives.

The DX7 Accident

The DX7's iconic "E.PIANO 1" patch — heard on thousands of pop records from 1983 to 1990 — was not carefully designed. It was a factory preset that happened to sound good because of Yamaha's particular implementation of the envelope generators. The six operators with their 32 algorithms create a parameter space so vast (155 billion possible combinations by one estimate) that most DX7 owners never programmed their own patches. They used the factory presets, and those presets defined the sound of an entire decade. The lesson for software engineers: sometimes the default configuration matters more than the configurability.

09 MIDI and Sequencing

MIDI — Musical Instrument Digital Interface — is a protocol from 1983 that refuses to die. It does not transmit audio. It transmits instructions: which note to play, how hard to press it, when to release it. Think of it as the difference between a WAV file and sheet music. MIDI is the sheet music, and it remains the universal language through which synthesizers, drum machines, DAWs, and controllers communicate.

Message Format

MIDI messages are byte sequences. The first byte is the status byte (high bit set, 0x80-0xFF), followed by one or two data bytes (high bit clear, 0x00-0x7F). The status byte encodes both the message type and the channel (0-15).

MIDI Message Structure 

  Status Byte:   1nnn cccc
                  │││ ││││
                  │││ └┴┴┴── channel (0-15)
                  └┴┴────── message type (0-7)

  Note On:   [0x90 + ch] [note] [velocity]
  Note Off:  [0x80 + ch] [note] [velocity]
  CC:        [0xB0 + ch] [ctrl] [value]
  Prog Chg:  [0xC0 + ch] [program]

  Example: Play middle C at max velocity on channel 0
    0x90  0x3C  0x7F
    │     │     └── velocity 127 (max)
    │     └──────── note 60 (middle C)
    └────────────── Note On, channel 0

MIDI's 7-bit data range (0-127) was generous in 1983 but limiting today. Velocity 0-127 means only 128 dynamic levels. Pitch bend uses two bytes for 14-bit resolution (16384 values), which is barely enough to sound smooth. MIDI 2.0 (2020) upgrades to 32-bit resolution, but adoption is glacial.

10 Audio Codecs: Compressing the Air

Uncompressed CD audio consumes 1,411 kilobits per second. A three-minute song is 31 megabytes. In 1991, that was a significant fraction of a hard drive. The quest to make audio files smaller without making them sound worse drove one of the most successful applications of psychoacoustic research: lossy audio compression. The algorithms do not preserve the signal — they preserve the perception of the signal.

The Format Landscape

  • PCM (WAV/AIFF): uncompressed, sample-for-sample faithful. The baseline everything else is compared against.
  • FLAC: lossless compression, typically 50-60% of original size. Prediction + residual coding. Bit-perfect reconstruction.
  • MP3 (MPEG-1 Audio Layer III): the codec that changed everything. Psychoacoustic model + modified DCT + Huffman coding. 128-320 kbps typical.
  • Ogg Vorbis: open-source lossy codec. Better quality per bit than MP3, especially at low bitrates. MDCT-based.
  • Opus: the modern champion. Hybrid of SILK (speech) and CELT (music). 6-510 kbps, ultra-low latency (2.5ms frames). Dominates VoIP, streaming, and WebRTC.

Psychoacoustic Model

Lossy codecs exploit how human hearing works:

  • Absolute threshold: sounds below ~0 dB SPL at any frequency are inaudible. Do not encode them.
  • Frequency masking: a loud tone at 1 kHz makes nearby frequencies (900-1100 Hz) inaudible. Encode them with fewer bits.
  • Temporal masking: after a loud transient, quiet sounds are masked for 5-20 ms. Before it too (pre-masking, ~5 ms).
  • Critical bands: the ear groups frequencies into ~25 Bark bands. Within a band, the loudest component masks the rest.

The codec transforms audio into frequency domain (MDCT), calculates masking thresholds per band, then quantizes each band just coarsely enough that the quantization noise stays below the masking threshold. You cannot hear what you cannot hear.

function wavHeader(sampleRate: number, channels: number,
                    bits: number, numSamples: number): ArrayBuffer {
  const blockAlign = channels * (bits / 8);
  const byteRate = sampleRate * blockAlign;
  const dataSize = numSamples * blockAlign;

  const buf = new ArrayBuffer(44);
  const view = new DataView(buf);
  const encoder = new TextEncoder();

  function writeStr(offset: number, s: string) {
    const bytes = encoder.encode(s);
    for (let i = 0; i < bytes.length; i++)
      view.setUint8(offset + i, bytes[i]);
  }

  writeStr(0,  'RIFF');
  view.setUint32(4,  36 + dataSize, true);
  writeStr(8,  'WAVE');
  writeStr(12, 'fmt ');
  view.setUint32(16, 16, true);           // PCM fmt size
  view.setUint16(20, 1,  true);           // PCM format
  view.setUint16(22, channels, true);
  view.setUint32(24, sampleRate, true);
  view.setUint32(28, byteRate, true);
  view.setUint16(32, blockAlign, true);
  view.setUint16(34, bits, true);
  writeStr(36, 'data');
  view.setUint32(40, dataSize, true);

  return buf;
}

fn wav_header(sample_rate: u32, channels: u16,
              bits: u16, num_samples: u32) -> Vec<u8> {
    let block_align = channels * (bits / 8);
    let byte_rate = sample_rate * block_align as u32;
    let data_size = num_samples * block_align as u32;
    let file_size = 36 + data_size;

    let mut h = Vec::with_capacity(44);

    h.extend_from_slice(b"RIFF");
    h.extend_from_slice(&file_size.to_le_bytes());
    h.extend_from_slice(b"WAVE");
    h.extend_from_slice(b"fmt ");
    h.extend_from_slice(&16u32.to_le_bytes());       // fmt chunk size
    h.extend_from_slice(&1u16.to_le_bytes());        // PCM
    h.extend_from_slice(&channels.to_le_bytes());
    h.extend_from_slice(&sample_rate.to_le_bytes());
    h.extend_from_slice(&byte_rate.to_le_bytes());
    h.extend_from_slice(&block_align.to_le_bytes());
    h.extend_from_slice(&bits.to_le_bytes());
    h.extend_from_slice(b"data");
    h.extend_from_slice(&data_size.to_le_bytes());

    h
}

Psychoacoustic Cheating

Every lossy audio codec is a study in principled cheating. The encoder analyzes what you can hear, what louder sounds mask, what frequencies your ears cannot resolve — and throws away everything else. At 128 kbps, MP3 discards roughly 90% of the original data. Opus at 96 kbps discards 93%. Yet in blind tests, most listeners cannot distinguish Opus at 128 kbps from the uncompressed original. The codec is not compressing sound — it is compressing perception, and perception has a much lower bandwidth than physics.

11 Web Audio API Internals

The Web Audio API is a high-level graph-based system for audio processing in the browser. Unlike the simple <audio> element, Web Audio gives you a modular routing architecture where you build signal chains by connecting nodes — oscillators, filters, gain controls, analyzers — into a directed acyclic graph. The browser's audio engine traverses this graph 128 samples at a time, mixing and processing in real time.

The Audio Graph

Everything starts with an AudioContext. It is the container for all audio operations — it owns the output, manages timing, and provides factory methods for creating nodes. The context has a sample rate (usually 44100 or 48000), a current time (audioCtx.currentTime), and a destination node that represents the speakers.

Common Nodes

  • OscillatorNode: generates waveforms (sine, square, sawtooth, triangle, custom). Has .frequency and .detune AudioParams.
  • GainNode: multiplies signal by a value. The .gain AudioParam supports automation (ramps, exponential curves).
  • BiquadFilterNode: second-order IIR filter. Types: lowpass, highpass, bandpass, notch, allpass, peaking, lowshelf, highshelf. Has .frequency, .Q, and .gain params.
  • AnalyserNode: provides FFT data and time-domain waveform for visualization. Does not modify the signal.
  • AudioBufferSourceNode: plays back an AudioBuffer (pre-loaded PCM data). One-shot — create a new one for each playback.
  • DelayNode: delays the signal by a specified time. Maximum delay set at creation.
  • ConvolverNode: convolution reverb using an impulse response buffer.
  • WaveShaperNode: applies a distortion curve (Float32Array transfer function).
  • AudioWorkletNode: custom DSP in a separate thread. Write your own processor class, register it, and process 128-sample blocks in real time. This is how you build effects that Web Audio does not provide natively.

The key architectural insight: AudioParams (like gain.gain or oscillator.frequency) are a-rate (audio rate, updated every sample) by default. This means you can modulate them smoothly with linearRampToValueAtTime() and setTargetAtTime(). No clicks, no zipper noise. The automation runs on the audio thread, not the main thread, so it is sample-accurate even when the UI is busy.

See Buffers for ring buffers and memory management patterns — the AudioWorklet processes fixed-size 128-sample blocks, which is a ring buffer pattern at its core.

12 Music Theory for Programmers

Equal temperament is a mathematical compromise that has dominated Western music for three centuries. The idea: divide the octave into 12 equally-spaced semitones, where "equally spaced" means each semitone is the same ratio above the previous one. That ratio is the twelfth root of 2, approximately 1.05946. Every interval is slightly out of tune compared to the pure harmonic ratios, but every key sounds equally (slightly) wrong, which means you can transpose freely. It is the ultimate engineering trade-off: perfection in any one key sacrificed for adequacy in all keys.

Scales and Chords

A scale is a subset of the 12 semitones. The major scale uses intervals [2,2,1,2,2,2,1] (whole and half steps). The minor scale: [2,1,2,2,1,2,2]. A chord is three or more notes from a scale played simultaneously. A major triad is root + major 3rd (4 semitones) + perfect 5th (7 semitones). A minor triad: root + minor 3rd (3) + perfect 5th (7).

For a programmer, the key insight is that all of music theory is modular arithmetic. Transposition is addition mod 12. Inversion is subtraction mod 12. The circle of fifths is just adding 7 mod 12 repeatedly: C, G, D, A, E, B, F#, C#, G#, D#, A#, F, C.

Twelve Notes Is a Compromise

The octave divides into 12 semitones because 12 is the smallest number where powers of 2^(n/12) closely approximate simple ratios. 2^(7/12) = 1.4983 is very close to 3/2 (the perfect fifth = 1.5). 2^(4/12) = 1.2599 approximates 5/4 (major third = 1.25). But none are exact. A "pure" fifth at 3/2 and a "tempered" fifth at 2^(7/12) differ by about 2 cents — imperceptible to most ears. Stack twelve fifths, however, and the error accumulates to 23.46 cents: the Pythagorean comma. Equal temperament distributes this comma evenly, making every fifth equally impure. Other cultures chose differently: Arabic maqam uses 24 quarter-tones, Indian classical music uses 22 shrutis, Indonesian gamelan uses non-octave scales. Twelve is not physics. Twelve is a cultural choice optimized for harmonic flexibility.

Sound is vibration, vibration is numbers, and numbers are something we know how to manipulate. Every synthesizer, every codec, every reverb algorithm is proof that understanding the physics of air pressure lets you conjure entire worlds from arithmetic.
